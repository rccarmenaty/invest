# Filed Fundamentals: Revenue-Confirmed Public-Time Earnings Surprises

## Thesis (1 paragraph)

Fund a genuinely new signal line: a public-time, revenue-confirmed GAAP earnings-surprise event, not another price-spike continuation. The primary hypothesis is deliberately binary and boring: after an eligible U.S. issuer publicly files a quarterly result, go long only when GAAP diluted EPS and GAAP revenue both produce a pre-registered positive seasonal surprise; enter at the next open and hold exactly 60 sessions. The event is measured first without positions, then tested in the existing fixed-horizon/random-admission structure only if the data object survives a point-in-time feasibility audit. This is not a claim that PEAD is already an investable edge. Promotion requires a positive after-cost h60 event study and portfolio result with non-negative median excess, positive trade-window excess versus matched SPY, no calendar year above the hard concentration cap, majority positive walk-forward folds, and survival at a predeclared cost stress. If the SEC data cannot be reconstructed as knowable at the time, or if the signal is only a mean/right-tail artifact, kill this line rather than buy an analyst tape or add a ranker.

## Mechanism (why edge should exist economically)

The mechanism is delayed processing of persistent operating information. A positive EPS surprise can be manufactured by expense timing, buybacks, tax effects, or one-off accounting. A same-direction revenue surprise is a crude but economically meaningful persistence check: the news is more likely to reflect demand or realized sales than merely an accounting-margin surprise. The predicted return path is therefore not “a stock that jumped keeps jumping.” It is that the market initially incorporates the headline but incompletely updates expectations about future earnings when the top line and bottom line jointly improve.

That mechanism has three testable implications. First, returns should be signed in the direction of the fundamental news after the public event, not merely correlated with the pre-event price spike. Second, the drift should be longer than the repository’s noisy +1/+5-session path and should survive a next-open entry. Third, the effect should weaken where information processing is already fast, such as heavily followed, liquid, and crowded names. Those are diagnostics, not knobs: they cannot be used to rescue a failed primary result.

The hypothesis is intentionally smaller than “fundamentals predict returns.” It is one event definition, one horizon, one portfolio admission control, and one economic mechanism. No text model, analyst-revision ranker, sector filter, volatility scaler, or stop policy belongs in this PRD.

## Literature anchors (3–8 citations with arXiv/DOI/URL + one-line relevance)

1. [Bernard and Thomas, “Post-Earnings-Announcement Drift: Delayed Price Response or Risk Premium?” (1989), DOI: 10.2307/2491062](https://doi.org/10.2307/2491062) — Foundational evidence and the delayed-response versus risk-premium dispute that this program must distinguish rather than assume away.
2. [Jegadeesh and Livnat, “Post-Earnings-Announcement Drift: The Role of Revenue Surprises” (2006), DOI: 10.2469/faj.v62.n2.4081](https://doi.org/10.2469/faj.v62.n2.4081) — Reports stronger drift when revenue and earnings surprises agree, which is the specific confirmation mechanism proposed here.
3. [Livnat and Mendenhall, “Comparing the Post–Earnings Announcement Drift for Surprises Calculated from Analyst and Time Series Forecasts” (2006), DOI: 10.1111/j.1475-679X.2006.00196.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1475-679X.2006.00196.x) — Analyst-forecast surprises outperform simple time-series surprises in their evidence; this is a direct warning that the free, reproducible data object may be weaker.
4. [DellaVigna and Pollet, “Investor Inattention and Friday Earnings Announcements” (2009; NBER WP 11683)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w11683) — Provides an economic limited-attention channel for delayed reaction and a reason to preserve public announcement timing rather than use revised daily fundamentals.
5. [Kim and Kim, “A Multifactor Explanation of Post-Earnings Announcement Drift” (2003), DOI: 10.2307/4126756](https://doi.org/10.2307/4126756) — A serious opposition result: factor adjustment can absorb apparent PEAD, so raw drift is not enough.
6. [Shumway, “The Delisting Bias in CRSP Data” (1997), DOI: 10.1111/j.1540-6261.1997.tb03818.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1540-6261.1997.tb03818.x) — Establishes why delisting and negative terminal returns must be retained in the point-in-time security universe.
7. [Harvey, Liu, and Zhu, “... and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns” (2016), DOI: 10.1093/rfs/hhv059](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhv059) — Requires a severe multiple-testing and t-statistic standard; this proposal has one primary rule, not a search over surprise variants.
8. [McLean and Pontiff, “Does Academic Research Destroy Stock Return Predictability?” (2016), DOI: 10.1111/jofi.12365](https://doi.org/10.1111/jofi.12365) — Documents out-of-sample and post-publication decay, making contemporary walk-forward evidence and realistic costs mandatory.

## Concrete strategy design (entries, ranking, exits, portfolio, costs assumptions)

**Data and event definition.** The primary event is the first public SEC filing for a quarterly result that contains an issuer-identifiable GAAP diluted EPS and GAAP revenue for the quarter. Prefer an 8-K Item 2.02 earnings-release filing when the required GAAP values are present in the original exhibit; do not silently substitute a later 10-Q. If the values are only available in the 10-Q, the event is a 10-Q filing event and is a separate, explicitly reported object—not an announcement-time result. Every event stores CIK, accession number, form, original artifact hash, fiscal period, `filed` date, SEC `accepted` timestamp, and the source location of each fact.

The primary surprise is a time-series, seasonal-random-walk surprise, chosen because it can be reconstructed from public filings without a licensed analyst-history tape. For issuer-quarter (t), define:

- `SUE_t = (EPS_t - EPS_{t-4}) / SD(EPS_j - EPS_{j-4})` over the preceding eight available quarter observations `j`;
- `SREV_t = (log(Revenue_t) - log(Revenue_{t-4})) / SD(log(Revenue_j) - log(Revenue_{j-4}))` over the same preceding eight observations.

The primary treatment is `SUE_t >= 0.5` **and** `SREV_t >= 0.5`, with at least eight prior quarters, comparable fiscal periods, finite denominators, and no use of a value first filed after the event. The 0.5 threshold, eight-quarter history, GAAP concepts, and log-revenue transformation are fixed before return inspection. No threshold grid is an acceptance path. EPS-only, revenue-only, and discordant-sign events are diagnostic controls specified in advance; they cannot replace the concordant treatment if it loses.

If the data audit shows that the required GAAP fields cannot be mapped consistently across issuers and fiscal calendars, the program stops. A later analyst-consensus version would be a new PRD, not an undocumented fallback. The literature’s stronger analyst-forecast result is a reason to be honest about this limitation, not permission to contaminate the experiment with a different tape.

**Universe and timing.** Use point-in-time Sharadar `TICKERS`/`ACTIONS` membership and CIK mapping, retain delisted securities and corporate actions, and restrict to U.S.-listed common shares with a predeclared minimum price and liquidity screen sufficient for the repository’s 5 bps/side primary assumption. Exclude ETFs, funds, ADRs, duplicate share classes, and securities without an unambiguous historical mapping. The screen is an execution boundary, not a ranker.

Use the SEC accepted timestamp as the public-time clock. To avoid intraday timestamp and close/open ambiguity, all eligible signals enter at the next observable session open, including filings accepted before the open and during market hours. If an announcement artifact is missing, late, amended, or only recoverable through a later restatement, exclude the event and count it in the data-coverage audit; do not backfill from current Company Facts values.

**Ranking and entries.** There is no ranking. The event is a binary fundamental filter. If multiple eligible issuers compete for a limited book, use the existing seeded random-admission lottery with a fixed seed and a fixed 20-slot cap. The lottery is a control for portfolio structure, not selection alpha. One issuer contributes at most one primary event per fiscal quarter and cannot open a second position while already held; these collision rules are fixed before the event study.

**Exits and path handling.** Use the existing `fixed-horizon` policy: next-open fill at entry and next-open exit after exactly 60 sessions. No hard stop, trailing stop, take-profit, ATR trail, volatility scaling, or early discretionary exit. The short-path +1/+5-session returns are diagnostics only. If a position is context-forced-closed, report it in full, FC-only, and non-FC books; stale terminal bars fail closed. The primary alpha claim must hold in the non-FC book and cannot be created by forced-close accounting.

**Costs and comparison.** Primary reporting is pre-tax after 5 bps per side, consistent with the settled research. Re-run the frozen rule at 10 and 20 bps per side as stress diagnostics, with no rule selection after seeing the grid; taxes remain secondary. Report raw return, after-cost return, same-date eligible-universe excess, factor-adjusted abnormal return where the required factors are point-in-time, and the repository’s trade-window SPY comparison using the exact entry-fill-to-exit-fill clock. Calendar buy-and-hold SPY is not the primary benchmark.

## How it differs from settled Phase 2 residual and from Round 1 proposals

This is a new information family: public GAAP accounting facts and their filing timestamp, rather than a naïve price-spike event. It does not reopen the frozen event → fixed-horizon → random-admission residual, does not alter its year gate, and does not add a ranker to rescue it. The fixed horizon and lottery are reused only as a neutral execution container after a new signal has passed a position-blind test.

It is not Quiet Drift or DAMB: neither an ID-ranked price continuation, a 52-week breakout, an absolute-market gate, nor a price-path package is being smuggled back in. It is not Round 1 Form-4: no insider-code interpretation, two-person cluster definition, or SEC ownership-tape thesis is required. It is also not “buy the same events with better stops.” If the fundamental filter does not produce an economic residual, the old price-event residual remains frozen.

## Why settled results (Steps 0–7) predict this might work

1. Steps 0–1 show that the old P&L was dominated by structure, stops, and forced-close artifacts. This program starts with a position-blind signal and keeps exits inert, so it does not attribute path mechanics to information.
2. Step 2 shows a horizon pattern: short paths were noisy while h20/h60 drift was more informative. A public accounting event has a credible slow-processing mechanism that maps to the longer horizon without requiring a stop.
3. Step 3 shows that the Core ranker did not beat the naïve spike control. This proposal has no price ranker; it tests whether a different information source changes the conditional return distribution at all.
4. Gate 1a’s h60 mean excess passed while its median remained negative. That combination is evidence for a measurable conditional mean, but not for an investable book. A revenue-confirmation filter is a named attempt to improve signal persistence and the median, not a license to celebrate the old mean.
5. The 2021/2022 weakness, 2020 concentration, and 2024 strength warn that any new line must pass calendar-year and walk-forward gates. A fundamental event might diversify the regime exposure; the experiment must demonstrate that rather than assume it.
6. Phase 2b failed both the leave-2020 economic-residual bar and matched-SPY trade-window comparison. Those failures rule out more work on the old residual. They make matched-SPY excess and year concentration hard gates for this new line from its first portfolio run.
7. Forced closes did not explain the old result. The new line still reports FC-separated books, preventing a fresh event claim from hiding the same operational artifact.

## Why it might fail (steelman opposition)

- PEAD may be a factor-model or risk artifact. Kim and Kim’s result means a raw signed drift is not sufficient; a factor-adjusted and matched-SPY result can kill the idea.
- The public free-data surprise is weaker than analyst-forecast surprise. Livnat and Mendenhall directly predict this risk. If the free time-series benchmark has little information, the program must die rather than quietly purchase a better tape.
- Revenue is not automatically quality. A same-direction revenue/EPS move can be a one-off acquisition, foreign-exchange effect, pull-forward, or a denominator artifact around losses. GAAP tag semantics and fiscal-quarter comparability may fail at scale.
- SEC timing is not the same as an earnings-call timestamp. A 10-Q may arrive after the market has already processed the release, and an 8-K exhibit may contain inconsistent non-GAAP presentations. A next-open event definition can therefore be too late or too noisy.
- Any observed mean could again be a small number of extreme winners, a 2020-like calendar cluster, or a market/industry beta burst. The median, year cap, matched-SPY measure, and issuer/date-clustered inference are designed to make that failure visible.
- The signal may have decayed. PEAD is heavily published, and McLean–Pontiff predicts weaker post-publication returns. A historical literature anchor is not evidence for 2019–2025 profitability.
- Liquidity and delisting are not bookkeeping details. Earnings shocks can gap through the next open; five basis points may be optimistic for small names. Missing delisting returns can manufacture apparent winners or hide losses.
- Multiple testing can happen even with good intentions: 8-K versus 10-Q, EPS versus EBITDA, several surprise scalings, thresholds, horizons, filters, and controls. Those are all frozen or excluded here. The first attractive variant is not allowed to become the primary one.

## Measurement plan (ordered experiments, sample design, stats)

1. **Freeze the PRD and data dictionary before returns.** Record the single primary treatment, source forms, concepts, timestamp rule, universe, liquidity boundary, h60 primary horizon, collision rules, 20-slot seed, cost grid, and all rejection gates. No analyst tape, language model, ranking, or stop experiment is in scope.
2. **F0: point-in-time data-feasibility audit, with no returns.** Build a bounded historical sample from the continuous window. For every candidate event, verify original accession retrieval, accepted timestamp, CIK-to-security mapping, fiscal-period identity, GAAP EPS/revenue comparability, amendment handling, and whether the value was available at the event. Produce coverage and exclusion counts by year and form. Require at least 1,000 eligible primary events and at least 100 distinct entry dates across the window; these are predeclared adequacy floors, not observed results. Failure ends the line.
3. **Parser and PIT leakage tests.** Unit-test that later amendments/restatements cannot alter an earlier event, that fiscal-quarter joins do not mix YTD and quarter-only values, that stock splits and CIK/ticker changes preserve identity, and that events accepted after the research cutoff are invisible. Test delistings and stale terminal bars with fixtures.
4. **Position-blind event study.** On the frozen primary event set, compute next-open signed returns and excess returns at h1, h5, h10, h20, and h60. The primary test is h60; the others are path diagnostics. Use same-date eligible-universe means, matched trade-window SPY, and factor-adjusted abnormal returns where available. Report mean, median, quartiles, hit rate, tail contribution, and per-calendar-year results. Use calendar-date clustering; use a two-way issuer/date estimator or block bootstrap if the repository can support it, and state exactly which inference is primary.
5. **Predeclared decomposition, not selection.** Report concordant EPS+revenue events against EPS-only, revenue-only, and discordant controls. Report liquidity, size, sector, event weekday, and announcement-time diagnostics only as heterogeneity checks. Do not choose a subgroup or threshold after inspecting h60.
6. **Chronological walk-forward event scoring.** Reuse the project’s annual entry-year folds and treat 2023–2025 as already viewed walk-forward evidence, not a virgin holdout. The signal has no fitted parameters; walk-forward means the event and universe rules are held fixed and each fold is scored only with information available before its event dates.
7. **Fixed-horizon portfolio test.** Only if F0 and the event study pass, run the binary primary event through next-open entry, 60-session exit, 20 slots, and seeded random admission. Run sequentially on the 16GB host. Publish full, non-FC, FC-only, by-year, fold, and collision/admission reports.
8. **Economic stress and falsification.** Apply 5/10/20 bps per side without selecting a winner; calculate the exact S2 trade-window SPY excess; repeat with entry delayed one additional open as a timing falsification; inspect whether the signal’s result is entirely the announcement gap, one year, one sector, or forced closes. The delayed-entry result is diagnostic and cannot substitute for the primary timing rule.
9. **Single terminal decision.** Issue GO/NO-GO for this named line. A passing event study with a failing portfolio is a diagnostic only, not permission to tune exits or rank events. A failing F0, median, S2, year, or cost gate pauses the line and requires a new explicit PRD for any materially different data or rule.

## Acceptance / rejection gates

**F0 data gate — all required.** At least 1,000 eligible primary events and 100 distinct entry dates; a reproducible original filing artifact and accepted timestamp for every primary event; no later-restated value used; valid point-in-time security mapping; and an auditable exclusion reason for every candidate that fails. If any of these cannot be proven, reject the line before return analysis.

**E1 position-blind signal gate — all required at h60.** The concordant treatment must have positive after-cost-equivalent excess versus the same-date universe, date-clustered `t >= 2.5`, and non-negative median excess. The matched trade-window SPY excess must also be positive in mean and non-negative in median. A positive raw mean with negative median, or a result that disappears after factor/SPY comparison, is a rejection. h1/h5 may be noisy; if h20/h60 does not carry the signal beyond the announcement path, reject the proposed 60-session drift thesis.

**P1 portfolio gate — all required.** At 5 bps/side, the fixed-horizon primary book and non-FC book both have positive mean expectancy and non-negative median expectancy; the primary non-FC book has positive matched-SPY trade-window excess; a majority of non-empty annual walk-forward folds has positive after-cost expectancy; and no entry year contributes more than the existing approximately 25% hard cap of total positive net profit. A non-positive total profit base fails the concentration gate rather than receiving an undefined pass. Forced-close-only profitability is an automatic rejection.

**P2 robustness gate.** The sign of mean expectancy and matched-SPY excess must not flip negative at 10 bps/side. The 20 bps run is a fragility report and cannot select a different universe or rule. The result must not depend on a single sector, a single announcement-time bucket, or the delayed-entry diagnostic. If it does, reject the broad primary claim and do not rebrand the subgroup as the strategy.

**Hard rejection conditions.** F0 leakage or inadequate coverage; current Company Facts values changing historical events; use of future fiscal facts; negative primary median; matched-SPY trade-window loss; year concentration above the hard cap; failure of the non-FC book; failure of majority walk-forward folds; or any rescue requiring a ranker, threshold search, alternative exit, or second autopsy. There is no “residual hope” track for this line after a hard-gate rejection.

## Implementation cost on this repo (files / new modules / data needs)

This is a medium-to-large research slice, but it is isolated from the frozen Phase 2 claim and should not edit the trading policy first.

- **New data adapter:** a point-in-time SEC filing/earnings-release ingest module, likely under `src/invest/adapters/`, with raw accession caching, SEC rate-limit/user-agent handling, accepted-timestamp preservation, CIK mapping, amendment lineage, and deterministic artifact hashes. The SEC EDGAR APIs and bulk archives are the starting source; current `companyfacts` responses must not be treated as historical vintages without an accession-level snapshot.
- **New domain/application objects:** an immutable filing event, quarterly GAAP fact, surprise calculation, exclusion reason, and event-study record under `src/invest/domain/` and `src/invest/application/`. Keep this separate from `phase2_report.py`; reuse generic cost, walk-forward, FC, and S2 helpers only where their semantics are identical.
- **New reporting driver:** a sequential fixture/report script under `fixtures/real-continuous/reports/`, analogous to `research_phase2.py`, that produces F0 coverage, event-study, portfolio, by-year, FC-segregated, cost-stress, and matched-SPY JSON/Markdown artifacts with full provenance.
- **Tests:** parser fixtures for 8-K/10-Q forms, amended filings, fiscal calendars, YTD-vs-quarter facts, split/ticker/CIK changes, late filings, missing facts, and stale terminal opens; pure tests for the surprise formula and frozen gates; end-to-end tests for next-open timing and seeded admission. Do not load the multi-GB continuous fixture in ordinary CI.
- **Data needs:** historical SEC filing archives or a reproducible SEC download cache, issuer submissions and XBRL/company facts, raw filing exhibits, a defensible CIK-to-Sharadar mapping, and the existing point-in-time SEP/ACTIONS/TICKERS plus SPY sidecar. If the archival SEC data cannot be obtained reproducibly, the work stops at F0 and does not become a simulated backtest.
- **Operational cost:** likely one data-ingestion implementation, one pure research/reporting implementation, and sequential multi-GB runs. No trading-code exit changes, no ranker, and no live deployment are authorized by this proposal.

## Confidence (0–100) and single sharpest risk

**Confidence: 61/100.** The mechanism is better matched to the evidence than another price-event package, and the revenue-confirmation hypothesis has a direct literature anchor. The confidence is not higher because PEAD is a heavily mined anomaly, the public time-series surprise is weaker than analyst-forecast SUE in published evidence, and the repository has not yet proven a PIT SEC earnings tape.

**Sharpest risk:** the historical public filing object may be impossible to reconstruct without semantic leakage—especially a release-time GAAP EPS/revenue pair that is both comparable across issuers and available before the next-open trade. If F0 fails, the thesis is not “data pending”; it is dead until a new PRD names and funds a different data source.

## Attacks on the other two archetypes

### A) Form-4 opportunistic clusters — Round 1 Codex Phase 3 as written

This is a legitimate new line, not a continuation, and it deserves more respect than a residual rescue. But it is the worse *next* use of this repository’s budget unless its point-in-time feasibility audit is already funded and clean. Form 4 has several independent failure surfaces: reporting obligation versus transaction date, SEC acceptance timing, amendments, issuer/insider/security identifiers, Table I versus derivative transactions, code-P interpretation, multiple purchases by the same insider, and the market’s knowledge of the filing at the proposed next-open entry. A two-person cluster adds another sparse-event and multiple-testing layer before a single return is measured. The result can easily become an expensive data-engineering project whose best-looking cluster definition is selected after seeing the event count or P&L.

The same economic tests would still be required: position-blind event study, after-cost h60 or predeclared horizon, median, year cap, matched-SPY trade-window excess, delisting handling, FC separation, and deflated inference. Form-4’s “insider conviction” narrative does not exempt it from those gates. It may be the right second line after the SEC infrastructure exists, but it is not automatically stronger merely because insiders sound more informed. Its sharpest advantage—closer-to-action information—can be destroyed by filing delay and sparse overlapping clusters.

### B) Rescue / rebrand residual price events

This is the most dangerous archetype **now**. Ranking, Quiet Drift, DAMB modules, vol-scaling, “drop 2020,” or a second concentration autopsy all reuse the same dead object while changing the language around it. The evidence is unusually clear: Gate 1a’s mean residual passed but median was negative; Phase 2 failed the hard year-concentration gate; K2 failed the leave-year economic-residual bar; S2 lost to matched SPY. A new ranker cannot retroactively make those measurements positive, and a new exit can only reopen a frozen claim by changing the object. It is the classic engineering sink: every knob has a plausible story, each run produces another mean, and the research budget never receives a terminal answer.

DAMB is particularly dangerous because market gates and trailing exits can turn beta exposure and path selection into apparent alpha. Quiet Drift is dangerous because ID ranking already failed monotonicity and “information discreteness” can become a narrative after the fact. “Just drop 2020” is not robustness; it is deleting the exact concentration failure that the program declared hard. The only defensible action is pause-default on the residual, with unrelated engineering allowed only if it cannot create an acceptance path.

### C) Honest beta / passive

This is the safest control and may be the correct business answer if the mandate is exposure rather than alpha. Productizing SPY/IWM or a transparent trend-following sleeve would stop the false claim that the current cross-section produces security-selection alpha. It is also not a research shortcut: TSMOM still requires an asset set, lookback, rebalance, leverage, turnover, financing, and crash-risk policy, each of which can be tuned. A beta product should be benchmarked as a product decision, not presented as a discovery from Gate 1a.

C is less dangerous than B because its claims are honest and its opportunity cost is visible. It is more dangerous than it looks if it becomes a way to avoid asking whether the project is trying to build a passive product or find an information edge. I would maintain it as a control/baseline and a possible fallback, not spend the next alpha-research budget tuning IWM versus SPY or a TSMOM lookback while the new fundamental event can be killed cheaply at F0 and E1.

**Ranking of danger now:** B is most dangerous because it defeats the freeze and manufactures false confidence from settled failures. A is second: potentially real, but a high data/engineering sink with sparse-event multiple testing. C is third: opportunity-cost risk, but low epistemic risk when labeled honestly.

## Final one-liner

I would spend the next research budget on a PIT SEC data audit followed by one revenue-confirmed GAAP earnings event study; the first experiment that proves or kills it is whether the frozen, public-time concordant event has positive h60 excess with a non-negative median and positive matched-SPY trade-window excess before any portfolio or ranker is built.
